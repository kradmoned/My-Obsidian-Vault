# information system vs information technology
he difference between an Information System (IS) and Information Technology (IT) is primarily one of **scope and focus**:

• **Information System (IS):** This is a broad concept that involves the interplay of human activity, software, and hardware elements

. Every IS must include a human activity that needs information, stored data, methods for input and output, and a process to transform the data into useful information. The **Information Systems Strategy** determines **what must be done** and identifies where IS can help the business. IS supports people's activities, such as storing and retrieving information, aiding communication, and controlling work

.

• **Information Technology (IT):** This refers specifically to the **technical infrastructure**—all the varieties of hardware and software elements used within an organization

. IT includes components like computers, networks, processors, and storage devices. The **Information Technology Strategy** is concerned with **hardware capabilities** and informs and enables the Information Systems Strategy

.

In short, the **IS** defines the entire functional and human structure necessary for business support, while **IT** is the technical toolset used to achieve that system


# Briefly explain why does it not matter whether an information system is real or exist in someones mind.
The source states that when discussing Information Systems (IS), it **does not matter** whether a system is real or exists only in someone's mind because the ultimate goal of "systems thinking" is achieving **understanding** of a problem.

Here is a brief explanation:

- **Focus on Utility, Not Reality:** Systems thinking is valuable because it serves as a tool to analyze and understand complex problems.
- **The Power of Perspective:** You have the choice to **see anything as a system**, regardless of whether or not it truly exists as a concrete, physical entity.
- **The Crux:** What matters is the **understanding you achieve** through treating the subject as a system.

Essentially, viewing a situation as an Information System is a subjective approach used to model and simplify reality to gain insight into the real-world situation or problem.

Here is how they relate, step-by-step:

1. **Business Strategy (The Driver):** This is at the top. It **drives and sets the goals**

. It determines _what must be done_

.

2. **Information Systems Strategy:** This level is **informed and enabled** by the Business Strategy

. It outlines _where Information Systems can help_ meet the overall business goals. The Business Strategy sets the goals that the IS Strategy must achieve

.

3. **Information Technology Strategy:** This is based on the IS Strategy and deals with the technical capabilities

. It focuses on the **hardware capabilities** and the specific technical requirements needed to implement the IS Strategy

.

Essentially, the Business Strategy dictates the "Why" and "What"

, which then shapes the IS Strategy ("Where IS can help"), which in turn determines the IT Strategy ("What hardware/technology is needed")
